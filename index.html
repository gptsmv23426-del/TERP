What Is TERP?

In one breath: the State of Texas will pay you a set amount toward a newer, cleaner diesel machine if you permanently retire an old, high-polluting one. That's the whole idea.

It's a real, established program, not a gimmick. TERP is run by the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ), it's been going since 2001, and the state has paid out over $1.4 billion to Texas businesses through it. The reason it exists is simple: old diesel engines pollute a lot, and Texas would rather help you upgrade than leave the old smoker running for another decade.

If you run construction or ag equipment in the right counties, it may help pay toward your next machine. Here's the deal in three parts:

1

You retire an eligible old machine for good.

2

The state puts a set amount of money toward a qualifying cleaner replacement.

How the Money Actually Works

The part that trips everybody up is the timing of the money. So here's a plain walk-through with a real example. Say you run a 2009 excavator. It still works, but it's old and burns dirty, and you've been thinking about upgrading anyway. Here's how TERP would play out:

  1. 1
    No money moves yet

    You apply and get approved first.

    Before you spend a dime, TCEQ confirms your project qualifies and tells you the grant amount you're working toward. You're not gambling. You know the number going in.

  2. 2
    Your money goes out

    You buy the replacement machine.

    This is your cash or financing, up front, for the full price, just like any equipment purchase. The grant doesn't pay the dealer. It comes back to you later.

  3. 3
    A required step

    You permanently retire the old machine.

    For a replacement grant, the old excavator gets destroyed so it can never run again, and you document it. That's the trade: the state's money is buying an old polluter off the road for good.

  4. 4
    Paperwork

    You submit proof.

    Receipts, photos, and proof the old machine was destroyed: the documentation TCEQ needs to release the grant. This is the part Justin walks you through so nothing gets kicked back.

  5. 5
    Money comes back to you

    The state reimburses you.

    A set grant amount comes back to you. For many machines that lands in the tens of thousands of dollars, based on TCEQ's table for your equipment type, horsepower, and age. Justin can run the number for your machine before you start.

Bottom line: you front the cost and wait for the check, but you know the grant amount before you buy. For a machine you were likely replacing anyway, that's money most contractors never think to ask for.

The Catch, and Why It Still Might Be Worth It

Here's what catches people off guard, and why each one usually isn't a dealbreaker.

It's a reimbursement, not a discount.

You buy or finance the machine first with your own money. The state pays you back after you finish the steps, including destroying the old machine. You need to be able to cover the full cost up front.

You find out your grant amount before you spend anything, so it's never a guess. If you were already going to finance the machine, the payment just comes back to you on top.

The amount is capped.

Grants are set by TCEQ's tables based on equipment type, horsepower, and age, or a share of the cost difference, whichever is less. It's real money, but it won't cover the whole machine.

It still adds up to a real check toward a machine you were going to buy anyway. Justin gets you the realistic figure up front so nothing surprises you later.

Funds are limited and first-come, first-served.

Demand usually runs well past the money available. Not everyone who applies gets funded, and being late or sloppy with the paperwork can cost you a round.

That's the best reason to start now instead of later. The contractors who get funded are the ones who were ready early with clean paperwork, and getting you there is Justin's job.

TCEQ makes the final call.

Nothing here is a guarantee. The state decides who qualifies and for how much.

You learn whether you qualify up front, so you're never betting a purchase on a maybe. Most of the work is simply doing it right the first time.

That's the full picture, not a sales pitch. Most guys who actually understand TERP decide it's worth a shot. One call tells you if you're one of them.

The Two Programs

There's more than one path. Justin will steer you to the right one, but here's the plain difference.

Rebate Grants Program

The simple path, best for most

First-come, first-served. Fixed grant amounts set by TCEQ tables based on equipment type, horsepower, and model year. Best for most single-machine contractors. Non-road equipment needs a 25+ HP engine. Includes a small-business set-aside for owners of 5 or fewer machines.

ERIG

Emissions Reduction Incentive Grants, for bigger projects

Competitive, not first-come. Scored on cost-per-ton of emissions reduced. Better for bigger or multi-machine projects. More paperwork, but potentially more money for the right project.

Not sure which fits, or whether either is worth it for you? That's exactly the kind of thing to ask Justin.

Straight Answers to What You're Probably Thinking

Honestly, this sounds like a lot of hassle.

The paperwork is the exact part that scares most people off, which is why Justin does that part with you. Your side is short: one conversation, a quick worksheet, and a few signatures. The TCEQ application, the grant tables, and the proof requirements are on him.

I figured this was only for big outfits.

It's not. There's a small-business set-aside built specifically for owners of five or fewer machines. Plenty of single-machine contractors and small ag operations get funded. If anything, this is more for you than for the big fleets.

My old machine still runs fine. Why would I replace it?

You don't have to. But if you were already going to upgrade in the next year or two, TERP can put a real chunk of money toward it that you'd otherwise never see. The trade is that the old machine gets retired for good, so it makes the most sense when you're ready to move on from it anyway.

What if I do all this and don't get the money?

Fair concern, and the honest answer is that nothing's guaranteed. TCEQ makes the call. But approval comes before you spend, so you're never risking the purchase itself. Justin won't point you at a long shot.

How long does the whole thing take?

It depends on the grant round and how fast you move. Some of it is on the state's timeline, not yours. Justin can give you a clear picture of what's open right now and how the timing would work for your project.
